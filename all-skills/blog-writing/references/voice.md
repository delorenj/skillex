# Authorial Voice

How Jarad's sentences actually move. The single biggest failure mode is writing *clean*. His voice isn't clean — it's a sharp mind talking out loud, hedging, swerving, and cussing under its breath. When a paragraph reads like it could go on a motivational poster, you've drifted into the synthetic zone. Rough it back up.

## Technique: The Doom-Swerve

The signature move. One sentence (or a tight run) that starts earnest and serious, detours through a hyper-specific apocalyptic image, then deflates hard into casual shrug + a practical takeaway. Earnest → cinematic doom → "eh, for now we're chill" → do this thing.

**Canonical exemplar** (closing line of *Somebody Sent Me a Trojan and Called It a Toy*):

> The robots that are making the world scarier, and in all likelihood, methodically drawing their plans to destroy us all - our bleached skulls shining in the intense floodlights of a hunter-killer before being crushed beneath the treads of the machines - are for the time being, pretty chill and all about catching these shady f#$&tards - so make your agent your personal bouncer and put it on the door.

Why it works:
- **Specificity sells the joke.** "bleached skulls... treads of the machines" is a rendered Terminator frame, not a generic "AI is dangerous." Vague doom isn't funny. Cinematic doom is.
- **You have to earn it.** The swerve only lands because the preceding paragraphs played it straight. Be sincere right up to the edge, then jump.
- **"for the time being"** does the cynical wink — the chill is explicitly temporary.
- **The deflation carries the CTA.** It doesn't moralize after the joke; the joke *is* the delivery vehicle for "put it on the door."
- Held together with em-dashes and commas in one breath, not chopped into neat sentences.

## Technique: Kill the Antithesis Hammer

The #1 "this was written by an AI" tell. The crisp, balanced, two-beat snap: **"You're not X. You're Y."** Period-snap-period. It feels engineered because it is. Use it almost never — maybe one real punch per post.

Dissolve it into one flowing, hedged, first-person sentence. The repair tools are **"so much as it is"** and **"less X and more Y."**

| Synthetic (avoid) | His voice (say) |
|---|---|
| "You're not detecting malice anymore. You're grading a disguise." | "It's not that I'm detecting malice, so much as it is grading its disguise." |
| "Scams used to have bad grammar and a single `index.html`; this one has a `Cargo.toml`." | "Back in the day, you pretty much could smell a scam by the shit code and bad grammar. But this here hides well among its crowded space of vibe-coded peers." |
| "It's not a footnote. It's the product." | "It isn't really a footnote so much as it is the entire product wearing a trenchcoat." |
| "Not a smash-and-grab. A houseguest who changes the locks." | "Less a smash-and-grab and more of a houseguest who lets himself in and changes the locks while you're in the kitchen making tea." |

Note the second example also **upgrades the observation**, it doesn't just rough up the grammar. "it has a `Cargo.toml`" → "it hides among its crowded space of vibe-coded peers" is a *better idea*, not just a looser sentence. De-synthesizing is half cadence, half sharper thought.

## Micro-tells

- **Deflation isn't clipped.** Snark ≠ snappy. "yeah, no." is still a hammer. He'd write "yeah, ok i'm not gonna do that" — verbal, rambling, lowercase i.
- **Symbol-censored cussing** is a feature: "f#$&tards" is funnier than the clean version *or* the literal one. Profanity otherwise rides in as texture ("the shit code," "a hell of a thing"), never as a shock beat parked on its own line.
- **Ironic virtue-lists.** Rule-of-three is allowed when the joke *is* the list: "I didn't dodge this because I'm smart, patient, and attentive" — set up purely to knock down.
- **Loose spoken grammar:** "back in the day," "this here," "pretty much," "on account of," run-ons stitched with *and / but / so*, trailing "..." Sentence fragments are fine when conversational, not when they're antithesis halves.

## Hard nos

- **No "picture this" / scene-setting openers.** No "Imagine you're sitting there..." No "Twenty minutes later:" cold-open theater. Walk in already pointing at the receipt. ("So a buddy drops a repo in my lap the other day.")
- **No manufactured suspense** to stretch thin material. If there isn't enough mystery, the piece is short. ~700 words beats a padded 2,000.
- **No load-bearing scaffolding** (scorecards, checklists, numbered "frameworks") unless the content genuinely demands it. Let commentary carry the lesson.

## Default shape

Receipts first → walk the reader through what you actually found, in spoken voice → pull back to the one idea you actually care about ("Now the part I actually care about") → land it with a doom-swerve or a flat, earned CTA ("Read it." / "Go check your config." / "put it on the door").
